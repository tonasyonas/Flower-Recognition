\subsection{Why Architectural Modifications Hurt}

The unmodified ResNet18 baseline beat every architectural modification we tried. That was not what we expected going in, and it took a while to come up with a satisfying explanation. Our best guess has three parts. The ImageNet-pretrained weights were calibrated for a standard $3 \times 3$ convolution: dilation samples across a wider area than those weights anticipate, and deformable convolution adds an offset predictor that starts at zero and needs more data than 1020 images to learn anything useful. The extra parameters and altered gradient flow also make the loss landscape harder, and the model never finds a solution as good as the baseline's. On top of that, the inductive biases of these operators are aimed at different problems (dilated for dense prediction, deformable for articulated objects); flower classification at this scale wants the opposite of what they offer, which is to keep the pre-trained features and adjust them gently.

\subsection{Why Each Convolution Variant Failed}

Each variant failed for a slightly different reason. \emph{Deformable convolution} (83.46\% MPC, 2.7 below baseline) was the closest of the four. On paper an offset-based sampler should help with the irregular geometry of petals, and we expected it to. The few-shot results show what went wrong: at $k = 1$ deformable trails the baseline by 8 points because the offset predictor cannot learn useful displacements from one example per class. \emph{Our hybrid} (dilated in \texttt{layer3}, deformable in \texttt{layer4}) lands at 81.34\%, between the two components. The two modifications did not compose synergistically; they compounded each other's optimisation difficulties instead. \emph{Depthwise-separable convolution} (59.71\% MPC) was the largest drop. Its kernel shape change makes copying pre-trained weights impossible, so \texttt{layer2}--\texttt{layer4} train from scratch. Fifty epochs over 1020 images is not enough to make up the difference. The 7$\times$ parameter saving does not justify a 26-point accuracy hit on this dataset.

\subsection{Regularization: Freezing Wins, MixUp Hurts}

Among the four regularizers, freezing the first two ResNet18 layers gave the largest gain (90.82\% MPC, $+4.71$ over baseline). The mechanism is the same one that makes VPT work: do not touch the pre-trained weights. Label smoothing added another $+3.29$ over baseline by softening the targets and reducing overconfidence. Combining label smoothing with strong augmentation gave less than label smoothing alone, so the two appear to compete for the same regularization slack.

MixUp's $-2.61$ result was the surprise. In most image classification settings MixUp improves generalisation, but at 10 images per class and 102 classes, mixed pairs span unrelated species more often than not. The resulting soft labels carry little class-discriminative information, and the model wastes capacity memorising blends instead of learning clean class boundaries. Strong augmentation alone produced no change relative to the baseline, which suggests Flowers 102 is already tolerant to mild geometric variation; the bottleneck is class identity, not pose.

\subsection{Why VPT-Deep Wins}

VPT-Deep does well for two reasons that reinforce each other. Its trainable parameter count is tiny (0.2\% of the backbone), which is freezing taken to its extreme and limits overfitting more aggressively than the Frozen ResNet18 does. The prompts themselves do useful work too: inserted into the input space at every Transformer layer, they act as task-specific queries that reshape what the frozen backbone attends to without changing what it computes. The pre-trained representations stay intact.

The few-shot curves confirm both effects. At $k = 1$ the VPT-Deep margin over the best CNN is 11 points; at $k = 10$ it shrinks to 1 point. Less data favours fewer parameters and more reliance on pre-training, which is exactly what VPT provides. The prompt length ablation (Figure~\ref{fig:prompt-length}) shows that longer prompts continue to help up to $p = 50$ (94.51\% val), so the 92.83\% MPC test result for our default $p = 10$ is not the ceiling for this method.

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}

\citet{nilsback2008} reported 72.8\% accuracy with a multiple-kernel SVM on hand-engineered features. Our VPT-Deep result (92.83\% MPC) sits above the 89.11\% FGVC average reported by \citet{jia2022vpt} for VPT-Deep on a ViT-B/16, though direct comparison with recent papers is hard because many use larger backbones (ViT-L/14) or different splits.

\subsection{Add-Ons, Variance, and Limitations}

We tried two add-ons on top of the best methods. The first was triplet loss, computed batch-hard on L2-normalised CLS embeddings, which raised VPT-Deep validation accuracy from 93.63\% to 94.02\% but did not change the test numbers. We suspect the auxiliary objective tightens embeddings around the validation distribution without generalising further. Getting the triplet term to train at all required computing it in float32 outside the mixed-precision autocast; without that we hit NaNs within a few epochs. The second was test-time augmentation, which improved every method by 0.4 to 0.9 MPC points at no extra training cost (Table~\ref{tab:tta}). VPT-Deep with TTA reaches 93.27\% MPC, our best test number.

The seed sweep (Section~\ref{sec:seed-sweep}) shows the relative ranking VPT-Deep $>$ Frozen $>$ Baseline holds at every seed and standard deviations stay below one point. VPT-Deep also has the smallest spread (0.49 std), which fits with the idea that its tiny trainable parameter count limits how much any single initialisation can affect the outcome.
