This project compared advanced CNN architectures, regularization techniques, and Visual Prompt Tuning for few-shot flower classification on Oxford Flowers 102. Across 13 model configurations and a few-shot sweep at $k \in \{1, 2, 5, 10\}$, four findings stand out. First, \textbf{VPT-Deep wins overall}, reaching 92.83\% mean per-class accuracy (93.27\% with TTA) while updating only 0.2\% of the backbone parameters, and the margin over every CNN grows as the training budget shrinks. Second, \textbf{architectural modifications hurt CNNs}: dilated, deformable, depthwise-separable, and our novel hybrid all underperform the unmodified ResNet18 because they disturb pre-trained features that 1020 training images cannot re-learn. Third, \textbf{the most useful CNN intervention is doing less}, not more: freezing the first two ResNet18 layers reaches 90.82\% MPC, beating label smoothing, MixUp, strong augmentation, and a 2$\times$ larger ResNet50; the same logic taken to the extreme motivates VPT. Fourth, \textbf{default regularizers do not always help}: MixUp dropped accuracy by 2.6 points at ten shots per class, and triplet loss raised validation but not test accuracy on VPT-Deep, both because the regularizer's assumptions break under extreme data scarcity.

In low-data fine-grained classification, optimisation stability and the preservation of pre-trained representations matter more than architectural innovation. Future work would extend the prompt length ablation past $p = 50$, test VPT on larger ViT-L/H backbones, and report variance across multiple seeds on the test set.
