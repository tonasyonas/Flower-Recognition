We compared CNN architectures, regularization techniques, and Visual Prompt Tuning on Oxford Flowers 102, with a few-shot sweep at $k \in \{1, 2, 5, 10\}$ across 13 model configurations.

VPT-Deep was the clear winner, reaching 92.83\% mean per-class accuracy (93.27\% with TTA) while updating 0.2\% of the backbone parameters. Its margin over every CNN grew as the training budget shrank.

Every architectural modification to ResNet18 hurt. Dilated, deformable, depthwise-separable, and our hybrid all came in below the unmodified baseline because the pre-trained features were too valuable to disturb when only 1020 training images were available to re-learn them. The most useful CNN intervention turned out to be doing less, not more: freezing the first two ResNet18 layers reached 90.82\% MPC, beating label smoothing, MixUp, strong augmentation, and a 2$\times$ larger ResNet50. The same logic taken to its extreme is what makes VPT work.

Default regularizers did not always help. MixUp dropped accuracy by 2.6 points at ten shots per class, and triplet loss raised VPT-Deep's validation accuracy without moving the test number. Both failures had the same root cause: the regularizer's assumptions break when data is this scarce.

In low-data fine-grained classification, optimisation stability and the preservation of pre-trained representations matter more than architectural innovation. Three follow-ups would push the numbers further: extending the prompt length ablation past $p = 50$, testing VPT on larger ViT-L/H backbones, and reporting variance across multiple seeds on the test set.
