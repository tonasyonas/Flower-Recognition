Our work pulls from four areas: transfer learning for fine-grained classification, advanced convolution operators, parameter-efficient adaptation of Vision Transformers, and few-shot learning. We trace the lineage that motivated each of our experiments rather than aiming for a survey.

\subsection{Transfer Learning and Fine-Grained Classification}

Adapting a large pre-trained backbone to a small target dataset usually means full fine-tuning: take the ImageNet weights and update everything end-to-end on the new task. \citet{yosinski2014transferable} found that lower layers learn general features that transfer across datasets, while higher layers become more task-specific. The practical recipe follows from that observation: start from ImageNet, optionally freeze the early layers, and fine-tune the rest.

Residual networks \citep{he2016resnet} added skip connections so gradients could bypass entire blocks, which made it possible to train much deeper networks. The ResNet family is what we use as the CNN side of this report. Vision Transformers \citep{dosovitskiy2021vit} showed that pure attention can match or beat CNNs on image classification when pre-trained on enough data, which is why our VPT experiments use a ViT-B/16.

Fine-grained recognition is harder than generic object recognition because inter-class differences are subtle and intra-class variation is large. Bilinear CNNs \citep{lin2015bilinear} capture pairwise feature interactions and improved fine-grained accuracy by computing the outer product of two streams of CNN features. The Oxford Flowers 102 dataset itself was introduced by \citet{nilsback2008}, who reported 72.8\% accuracy with a multiple-kernel SVM combining SIFT, HOG, and HSV descriptors. The paper established mean per-class accuracy as the primary evaluation metric, which we adopt to handle the class-imbalanced test split.

\subsection{Advanced Convolution Operators}

Three lines of work generalise the standard $3 \times 3$ convolution. Each was motivated by a specific limitation.

\textbf{Dilated convolutions} were introduced by \citet{yu2016dilated} for dense prediction. They insert zeros between kernel weights to enlarge the receptive field without adding parameters. \citet{chen2017deeplab} used them in DeepLab to keep spatial resolution while still capturing wider context. Wider context is what we want from the deeper layers of ResNet18, so that is where we test the operator.

\textbf{Deformable convolutions} \citep{dai2017deformable} replace the rigid sampling grid with one that adapts per pixel. A small auxiliary branch predicts $2 \cdot k^2$ offsets per spatial location, and the main convolution samples at the shifted positions. The original paper showed gains on object detection and segmentation, where target objects deform significantly. Flowers vary a lot in petal arrangement and pose, so it seemed worth trying.

\textbf{Depthwise-separable convolutions} factor a $3 \times 3$ convolution into a per-channel spatial filter followed by a $1 \times 1$ mixing. \citet{howard2017mobilenets} used the factorisation in MobileNet to cut compute by about $8\times$ for mobile deployment. \citet{chollet2017xception} pushed further with Xception and showed depthwise-separable can match or beat standard convolution on ImageNet given enough capacity. Our hope was that the smaller parameter count would reduce overfitting at 10 examples per class.

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Tuning}

Full fine-tuning updates every backbone parameter and stores a separate copy of the model per downstream task. Lighter alternatives have shown up over the past few years. \emph{Linear probing} freezes the backbone and trains only a linear classifier on top of its features. \emph{BitFit} updates only the bias terms. \emph{Adapter} layers \citep{jia2022vpt} insert small bottleneck modules between Transformer layers and train only those modules. Each method trades plasticity for parameter efficiency.

\textbf{Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT)} \citep{jia2022vpt} brings the prompt-tuning idea from language models to vision. Instead of modifying the network, VPT inserts learnable prompt vectors into the Transformer's input space. The backbone stays frozen and only the prompts plus the classifier head are trained. There are two variants: shallow injects prompts at the first Transformer layer only, deep injects fresh prompts at every layer. The original paper found that VPT-Deep beat full fine-tuning on 20 of 24 downstream tasks while training less than 1\% of the parameters, with the gap being largest in low-data regimes. That last point is why we picked VPT as the main alternative to ResNet18 fine-tuning on Flowers 102.

A 2025 follow-up, \emph{Prompt-CAM} \citep{chowdhury2025promptcam}, repurposed the prompts as an interpretability tool: each class-specific prompt's attention map highlights the discriminative regions of the input. We do not implement Prompt-CAM, but it is an obvious next step now that we already train VPT models.

\subsection{Few-Shot Learning}

Few-shot learning is the problem of generalising from very few labelled examples per class. Three families of methods dominate the literature.

\emph{Metric learning} embeds inputs into a feature space where same-class examples are close and different-class examples are far apart, then classifies new examples by nearest neighbour. \citet{vinyals2016matching} introduced Matching Networks, which compute attention-weighted nearest-neighbour predictions over a small support set. \citet{snell2017prototypical} simplified the idea with Prototypical Networks: each class is the centroid of its support embeddings, and queries are classified by Euclidean distance to those centroids. The triplet loss we use to fine-tune VPT-Deep is from the same family; it pulls same-class embeddings together and pushes different-class embeddings apart.

\emph{Meta-learning} treats each few-shot task as a training instance and optimises for fast adaptation. MAML \citep{finn2017maml} learns an initialisation from which a few gradient steps on a new task produce a good model. These methods need episodic training across many tasks, which is not directly applicable to a single dataset like Flowers 102, so we do not implement them.

\emph{Transfer learning baselines} just fine-tune a pre-trained backbone on the few-shot training set. Recent work has shown that strong pre-training plus standard fine-tuning often matches or beats more elaborate few-shot methods. Our results agree: VPT-Deep with no episodic training wins at every $k$ we tried.

\subsection{Regularization for Small Datasets}

Standard cross-entropy training with one-hot targets pushes the network toward overconfident predictions, which leads to overfitting when data is scarce. \textbf{Label smoothing} \citep{szegedy2016rethinking} replaces the one-hot target $y$ with $\tilde{y}_i = (1 - \alpha) y_i + \alpha / K$, where $K$ is the number of classes. This bounds the maximum softmax probability the model can predict and slightly improves calibration in addition to acting as a regularizer.

\textbf{MixUp} \citep{zhang2018mixup} trains on convex combinations of pairs of inputs and labels: $\tilde{x} = \lambda x_i + (1-\lambda) x_j$, $\tilde{y} = \lambda y_i + (1-\lambda) y_j$, with $\lambda \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \alpha)$. Training on these mixed examples enforces locally linear behaviour between training points and reduces memorisation. \textbf{CutMix} \citep{yun2019cutmix} is a follow-up that cuts a rectangular patch from one image and pastes it onto another, with the label mixed proportionally to the patch area. We use the official \texttt{torchvision} v2 implementation of MixUp.

\textbf{Triplet loss} \citep{schroff2015facenet} trains an embedding by pushing the distance from an anchor to a positive (same class) sample below its distance to a negative (different class) sample by some margin. \citet{hermans2017triplet} proposed batch-hard mining, which picks the hardest positive and hardest negative within each batch and gives much stronger training signal than random or semi-hard sampling. Our triplet experiments use batch-hard mining.
